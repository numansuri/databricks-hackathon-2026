Referral Copilot visual exploration

Seven UI directions with more depth, real product texture, and map-first energy

These are disposable design boards for the doctor and hospital scheduling product. The newer directions push harder on fundamentals: hierarchy, density, map clarity, adaptive layout, and a more human-made visual signature.

Command Dispatch Network Ledger Exchange Atlas Fluid
Hierarchy before decoration Every direction gives one dominant decision surface, then uses smaller cards for evidence and alternatives.
Map layers, not map clutter Markers, routes, filters, and callouts are separated so the map can hold data without becoming wallpaper.
Fluid from the inside out New layouts are built around panes and modules that can collapse independently across doctor and hospital views.
Human-made texture More tactile edges, ledger marks, imperfect map texture, and product-specific copy replace generic glossy AI styling.
01

Clinical Command Center

Polished and trustworthy, but less flat. The map becomes a layered work surface, with stronger evidence cards and a schedule rail that feels ready for real hospital operations.

  • Best if we want Databricks credibility, clean data signals, and a serious clinical feel.
  • Depth comes from frosted panels, raised map metrics, route lines, and compact scheduling modules.
  • Good default direction for the real app because it upgrades the current style without a hard reset.

02

Referral Dispatch

More kinetic and high-stakes. This turns scheduling into an operations cockpit where hospitals can request coverage and doctors can accept shifts with route, urgency, and demand context.

  • Best for demos because it feels energetic and visibly different from standard health dashboards.
  • Great for two-way scheduling: bids, approvals, and coverage gaps are front and center.
  • Riskier as a default because the darker cockpit tone may feel intense for routine use.

03

Care Network Studio

Lighter, more humane, and easier to live in all day. The scheduling flow feels like a shared care network rather than a queue of tickets, with warm surfaces and visible relationship context.

  • Best if we want the app to feel approachable for onboarding and repeated doctor use.
  • Strong fit for context updates because the chat panel feels conversational, not transactional.
  • Could blend nicely with the current UI while adding personality through map texture and roster cards.

04

Signal Ledger

A more tactile field-work direction: less polished SaaS shine, more dispatch notebook. It feels like evidence, calls, and approvals are being logged in a real operational record.

  • Best if we want to avoid the generic AI dashboard look and feel more grounded in field medicine.
  • Uses restrained paper texture, stamps, ledger rows, and imperfect route marks without sacrificing clarity.
  • Strong for auditability: every schedule decision has a visible trail of signals and human approval.

05

Hospital Exchange

A denser marketplace-like control surface for two-way scheduling. Doctors and hospitals can both request, counter, and confirm without losing sight of the map.

  • Best if the hospital view is just as important as the doctor view.
  • Leans into queues, row states, and clear status language instead of decorative cards.
  • Feels more enterprise and less generated because the layout is shaped by negotiation workflow.

Atlas Console

A map-native direction with a real app shell: navigation rail, layer stack, selected facility, and inspector. It is less glossy and more like a serious spatial planning tool.

  • Best if Google Maps should feel like the product, not a background panel.
  • Good for scaling into datasets: layers, clusters, capability filters, and source confidence.
  • The nav rail makes desktop feel more mature while still collapsing cleanly on mobile.

07

Fluid Pane System

A modern adaptive layout direction. Instead of one big dashboard that shrinks badly, the product is made of panes that reflow around the task: context, decision, map, and schedule.

  • Best if we want the same design language to support onboarding, doctor, and hospital screens.
  • Uses responsive modules and container-based panes so dense information can stay legible.
  • Feels less AI-made because the system is pragmatic: every panel has a job and a clear priority.
